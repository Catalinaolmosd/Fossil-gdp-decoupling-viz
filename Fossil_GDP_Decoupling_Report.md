# Fossil Fuel Dependency and Economic Growth Report
*An Empirical Assessment of Decoupling Patterns Across Countries*

---

## Abstract
This report investigates whether economic growth can be decoupled from fossil fuel consumption across nations. Using harmonized global energy and economic data, we analyze structural, policy, and population factors affecting fossil fuel dependence. While relative decoupling is observed in high-income countries, absolute reductions remain rare, highlighting the need for targeted policy interventions and structural reforms.

---

## Introduction
Decoupling economic growth from fossil fuel use is a central goal of global climate policy. While efficiency improvements and economic shifts have allowed some high-income nations to stabilize or reduce fossil fuel use, global consumption continues to rise, driven by industrialization, population growth, and structural dependencies. This study evaluates whether higher incomes are systematically linked to lower fossil fuel dependence or if policy and structural factors dominate.

---

## Data and Methodology

### Data Source
- Fossil fuel consumption (total and per capita)  
- GDP per capita (constant USD)  
- Population  
- Fossil fuel share of primary energy  

**Temporal Scope:** Cross-sectional analysis focuses on 2022; longitudinal data spans 1990 to the most recent available year.

### Visualization Strategy
- **Figure 1:** Population, energy mix, and total fossil use.  
- **Figure 2:** Dynamic growth-energy trajectories over time.

---

## Key Findings

### Population and Energy Mix (Figure 1)
- Population size alone does not predict fossil fuel dependence.  
- Large countries with moderately high fossil fuel reliance contribute disproportionately to global consumption.  
- Structural scale effects outweigh national efficiency metrics in shaping outcomes.

### Economic Growth and Fossil Fuel Trajectories (Figure 2)
- **Industrialization phase:** Rising incomes strongly correlate with higher per-capita fossil fuel use.  
- **High-income plateau:** Efficiency gains stabilize, but do not substantially reduce, fossil fuel use.  
- **Structured outliers:** Norway shows high income with low fossil fuel consumption, thanks to hydroelectric power and long-term policy planning.

### Discussion
- Relative decoupling is common, but absolute reductions are rare.  
- Outsourcing, saturation, and persistent reliance on fossil fuels limit automatic decoupling.  
- Effective decoupling requires structural reforms, strong governance, and targeted energy policies.  
- Norway exemplifies how domestic low-carbon strategies combined with strategic energy planning can reduce fossil fuel dependence while maintaining growth.

---

## Conclusion
Economic growth alone does not ensure lower fossil fuel use. Structural factors, policy interventions, and energy system design play a dominant role. Observed patterns suggest that real reductions in fossil fuel consumption require deliberate governmental action and careful assessment of both domestic and global energy contexts.
